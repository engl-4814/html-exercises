Knee Rehabilitation for Health and Safety

This article highlights key components to strengthen and stretch your knee muscles to promote recovery and prevent future injuries. If you’re recovering from a knee injury or surgery, a well-structured knee conditioning program can help you regain strength, flexibility, and range of motion. The following content are some key points to consider:

Strength Training:

Strengthening the muscles that support your knee is crucial. Strong muscles reduce stress on the knee joint and help absorb shock. Focus on the following muscle groups:

  • Hamstrings

  • Quadtracepts

  • Abductors & Adductors

  • Gluteus medius & Gluteus maximus

Flexibility

Stretching the muscles you strengthen is essential for restoring range of motion and preventing injury. After strength exercises, gently stretch to reduce muscle soreness and maintain flexibility.

Warmup

Before starting exercises, warm up with 5 to 10 minutes of low-impact activity like walking or stationary cycling.

Exercises:

    Helcord Stretch:

  1. Stand facing a wall with your unaffected leg forward and a slight bend at the knee.

  2. Your affected leg should be straight and behind you, heel flat and toes slightly pointed inward.

  3. Press your hips forward toward the wall, keeping both heels flat on the floor.

  4. Hold this stretch for 30 seconds, then relax for 30 seconds. Repeat.

    Standing Quadtracepts Stretch:

  1. Hold on to the back of a chair or a wall for balance.

  2. Stretch the front of your thigh by bending your knee and bringing your heel toward your buttocks.

  3. Hold for 30 seconds, then relax. Repeat.

Injury Breakdown:

1. Anterior Cruciate Ligament (ACL) Injury:

The ACL is one of the ligaments connecting the shinbone to the thighbone. ACL injuries often occur during sports that involve sudden changes in direction (e.g., basketball, soccer).

2. Fractures:

The bones of the knee, including the kneecap (patella), can break due to falls or accidents. Osteoporosis can weaken bones, making them susceptible to fractures even from minor trauma.

3. Torn Meniscus:

The meniscus is rubbery cartilage that acts as a shock absorber between the shinbone and thighbone. Sudden twisting of the knee while bearing weight can tear the meniscus, leading to pain and limited mobility.

4. Sprained or Strained Knee Ligaments and Muscles:

These injuries often result from trauma or overuse. Ligaments and muscles can become sprained or strained, causing pain, swelling, and instability.

5. Tendonitis:

Inflammation of tendons around the knee can cause discomfort. Overuse, repetitive movements, or underlying conditions contribute to tendonitis
